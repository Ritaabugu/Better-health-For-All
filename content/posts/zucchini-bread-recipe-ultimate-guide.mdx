Summer zucchini piles up fast, and if you've got more than you know what to do with, this zucchini bread recipe is exactly where it should go. It makes two full loaves, it's moist enough to stay soft for days, and you'd genuinely never guess there's vegetable hiding inside it.

No mixer required. No fancy technique. Just two bowls, a box grater, and a little patience while it bakes.

### **Why This Zucchini Bread Recipe Works So Well**

The magic here is in the ratio. Three cups of grated zucchini against three cups of flour keeps the loaf moist without turning it dense or soggy. Measure the zucchini instead of eyeballing it. Too much and the extra moisture weighs the bread down.

Baking soda and baking powder working together give this bread its rise, while a full tablespoon of vanilla and two teaspoons of cinnamon carry the flavor. The zucchini itself barely announces its presence. It just makes everything softer.

Lining the pans with parchment paper is a small step that pays off later. It makes lifting the finished loaves out almost effortless, and combined with a buttered pan underneath, the paper stays put instead of sliding around while you pour in the batter.

### **What You'll Need for This Zucchini Bread Recipe**

#### **Dry ingredients:**

* 3 cups all-purpose flour
* 1 1/2 teaspoons baking powder
* 1 teaspoon baking soda
* 1 teaspoon fine sea salt
* 2 teaspoons ground cinnamon

#### **Wet ingredients:**

* 3 large eggs, room temperature
* 1 cup extra light olive oil, or canola or vegetable oil
* 1 1/2 cups granulated sugar
* 1 tablespoon vanilla extract

#### **The star:**

* 3 cups grated zucchini, from about 1 pound or 2 medium zucchini

#### **Optional mix-ins:**

* 1 cup chopped walnuts or pecans
* Chocolate chips, raisins, or dried cranberries also work

### **How to Make This Zucchini Bread Recipe**

**1. Prep your pans and oven.** Preheat the oven to 350°F with a rack in the center. Generously butter two 8x4 inch metal loaf pans, then line the bottom of each with parchment paper. The butter underneath helps the parchment stay put, and it makes the finished loaves easy to lift out later.

**2. Whisk the dry ingredients**. In a large bowl, combine the flour, baking powder, baking soda, salt, and cinnamon. Whisk until fully blended. This step also aerates the flour, so there's no need to sift anything separately.

**3. Whisk the wet ingredients**. In a second large bowl, whisk together the room temperature eggs, oil, sugar, and vanilla extract until it's smooth and well combined.

**4. Combine wet and dry**. Add the flour mixture into the wet ingredients and whisk until completely smooth, with no lumps left. The batter will turn thick at this stage. It takes a bit more effort to mix, but it comes together.

**5. Grate the zucchini**. Trim the ends off your zucchini, then grate on the large holes of a box grater. Measure out exactly 3 cups. If you end up with extra, save it for zucchini fritters instead of adding it to the batter.

**6. Fold in the zucchini**. Add the grated zucchini to your batter and fold gently until every strand is coated. Don't overmix here, just enough to combine.

**7. Add your mix-ins, if using**. Fold in chopped walnuts or pecans, chocolate chips, raisins, or dried cranberries. Use one, use a combination, or skip this step entirely for a classic loaf.

**8. Divide the batter evenly**. Split the batter between your two prepared pans. A kitchen scale makes this easy if you want both loaves to bake at the exact same rate, though eyeballing it works fine too.

**9. Bake**. Place both pans in the center of your preheated oven and bake for 50 to 60 minutes. You'll know it's done when the top looks puffed and a toothpick inserted into the center comes out clean.

**10. Cool completely**. Let the loaves rest in their pans for 10 to 15 minutes, then transfer to a wire rack. Remove the parchment paper at this point so the bottom cools evenly instead of steaming. Let the bread cool fully before slicing, since cutting into it too hot can leave it gummy.

### **Tips for the Best Zucchini Bread Recipe**

A few small details make a real difference here.

Room temperature eggs blend more evenly into the batter than cold ones straight from the fridge. Pull them out ahead of time if you remember, though a quick warm water bath works in a pinch.

Measuring the zucchini matters more than it seems like it should. Three cups is the sweet spot. Go over that and the extra water content can leave your loaf underbaked in the middle even after a full hour in the oven.

A serrated knife gives you the cleanest slices once the bread has cooled. A regular knife tends to compress the loaf instead of cutting through it neatly.

And don't rush the cooling. The bread firms up as it sits, and slicing too early gets you a gummier texture than you're after. Ten to fifteen minutes in the pan, then a full cool on the rack, gets you the best result.

### **Storing This Zucchini Bread Recipe**

This loaf genuinely gets better with a day of rest. It stays moist and tender for several days when wrapped tightly and left at room temperature, or stored in an airtight container.

If you want to keep it longer, wrap each loaf in plastic wrap and freeze it for up to three months. Thaw at room temperature before slicing, and it tastes nearly as good as the day you baked it.

Slicing before freezing is worth considering too, especially if you know you'll only want a piece or two at a time. Wrap individual slices separately so you can pull just what you need straight from the freezer, without thawing the whole loaf every time a craving hits.

### **Variations Worth Trying**

This zucchini bread recipe holds up well to swaps. Yellow squash works just as well as zucchini if that's what you've got on hand. Chopped pecans add a buttery crunch, while walnuts bring a slightly earthier bite.

If you want a dessert leaning version, fold in chocolate chips instead of nuts. For something a little tart, dried cranberries or raisins add pockets of sweetness throughout the loaf. Mix and match based on what's already in your pantry.

You can also go halfway between all of them. A combination of chocolate chips and chopped walnuts gives you a little crunch and a little sweetness in the same slice, without leaning too far in either direction. There's no wrong combination here as long as you stick close to one cup of mix-ins total, so the batter to add-in ratio stays balanced.

### **Common Mistakes to Avoid**

Skipping the parchment paper is an easy one to regret. Without it, the loaves can stick to the pan even after buttering, and you risk tearing the bottom crust trying to release them.

Overmixing once the zucchini goes in is another common trip up. Fold it in gently just until combined. Stirring aggressively at this stage can make the finished loaf tougher than it needs to be.

Cutting into the bread while it's still hot is tempting, especially with a kitchen that smells this good. But warm bread compresses under the knife instead of slicing cleanly, and the texture turns slightly gummy. Give it the full cooling time before you dig in.

Finally, don't skip measuring the zucchini. It's easy to assume more is better with a vegetable this mild, but going over three cups adds enough extra moisture to leave the center underbaked even at the full 60 minutes.

### **Frequently Asked Questions**

**Can I use yellow squash instead of zucchini?** Yes. Yellow squash works just as well and adds a similar texture with a slightly milder flavor.

**Can I use cucumber instead?** No. Cucumber has a completely different water content and texture, and it won't bake the same way in this batter.

**Does this zucchini bread recipe need to be refrigerated?** Not right away. It stays fresh at room temperature for several days when wrapped tightly, though refrigeration can extend that if your kitchen runs warm.

### **Conclusion**

This zucchini bread recipe turns an overflowing garden or a farmers market haul into two loaves worth holding onto. It's moist, it's simple enough for a weeknight bake, and it disappears fast once it's out of the oven.

Slice it warm with coffee, save a loaf for the freezer, or share it with someone who claims they don't like zucchini. They'll probably never guess what's actually in it.
