There are two kinds of peach crisp people. The ones who want a mountain of buttery, oat studded topping, and the ones who think the fruit should be the star. This peach crisp recipe is for the first group. If you've ever scraped the crumb off the top of someone else's crisp and left the soggy fruit behind, you already know which side you're on.

This is not a fussy dessert. You don't need a mixer, you don't need to chill any dough, and you don't need more than one bowl for each component. What you do need is ripe peaches, a shallow baking dish, and about ten minutes of hands on time before the oven does the rest.

It's the kind of recipe you can throw together on a random Tuesday because you had peaches going soft on the counter, or scale up for a backyard dinner when you want something that looks like effort but really isn't. Either way works. This crisp doesn't ask you to choose between easy and impressive.

### **Why This Peach Crisp Recipe Gets the Topping Right**

Most crisp recipes treat the topping like an afterthought. This one doesn't. The trick is using a shallow one and a half to two quart baking dish instead of a deep 8x8. A shallow dish spreads the peach layer thinner, which means the topping doesn't get stacked on top of itself. It stays spread out, and spread out is what gets crispy.

If you genuinely prefer a deep dish, fruit heavy crisp, you can still use an 8x8 pan. Just know you're trading crunch for a softer, more custardy result. Neither is wrong. But this peach crisp recipe was built around maximum crisp, so the shallow dish is the move.

There's also something to be said for restraint in the fruit filling itself. A lot of crisp recipes drown the peaches in sugar and call it a day. This one uses cornstarch and lemon juice to do real work, thickening the juices and cutting the sweetness so the peaches still taste like peaches. Sweet, but not syrupy. Bright, not flat.

And the cinnamon shows up twice, once in the filling and once in the topping, which ties the whole dessert together instead of leaving the spice to just one layer. Small choice, noticeable difference.

### **What You'll Need for This Peach Crisp Recipe**

#### **For the Peach Filling**

* 3 to 3 1/2 cups sliced peaches
* 3/4 cup granulated sugar
* 2 tablespoons cornstarch
* 1/2 teaspoon fine sea salt
* 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon
* Juice of 1 lemon
* 1 teaspoon vanilla extract

#### **For the Crisp Topping**

* 3/4 cup packed light brown sugar
* 1/2 cup all-purpose flour
* 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon
* 3/4 cup old-fashioned rolled oats
* Scant 1/2 teaspoon fine sea salt
* 1/3 cup unsalted butter, room temperature, cut into 1/2 inch cubes

The room temperature butter matters here. Cold butter won't incorporate the same way, and you'll end up with dry, floury pockets instead of that clumpy, craggy topping you're after.

### **How to Make This Peach Crisp Recipe**

Start with the peaches. Toss your sliced peaches with the sugar, cornstarch, salt, cinnamon, lemon juice, and vanilla in a large bowl. Stir until every slice is coated. The cornstarch and sugar need full contact with the fruit to thicken properly as it bakes, so don't rush this step.

Set the peaches aside and make the topping. In a separate bowl, whisk together the brown sugar, flour, cinnamon, oats, and salt. Add the room temperature butter cubes, then work them into the dry mixture with your fingers. Keep rubbing until the mixture no longer looks dry and starts clumping into small, buttery crumbs.

Pour the peach filling into your shallow baking dish. Sprinkle the crisp topping evenly over the top, making sure it reaches the edges so nobody gets a topping free bite. Bake at 375°F for 30 minutes, until the top is deep golden brown and the peach filling is bubbling visibly at the sides.

Here's the part almost everyone skips. Let the crisp cool for 30 to 45 minutes before serving. This isn't optional if you want a filling that holds together instead of running all over the plate. The peaches keep thickening as they cool, and that patience pays off in texture.

Serve warm with a scoop of vanilla ice cream. It's not a rule, exactly. But it's close to one.

### **Tips for the Best Crisp Topping**

A few things separate a good version of this dessert from a forgettable one.

Room temperature butter is non negotiable. It's the difference between a topping that clumps beautifully and one that stays sandy and separate no matter how long you work it.

Pan shape changes everything. A shallow dish gives you crunch. A deeper dish gives you a softer, more filling forward bake. Pick based on what you actually want to eat, not what's just sitting in your cabinet.

Don't skip the resting time. Pulling a crisp straight from the oven feels satisfying, but the filling needs time to set. Ten minutes in, it will still be loose. Give it the full 30 to 45 minutes and you'll notice the difference immediately.

Ripe peaches make or break this. If your peaches are still firm and a little green at the pit, they'll bake up tough instead of tender. Let them ripen on the counter for a day or two if needed before starting.

### **Common Mistakes to Avoid**

Skipping the cornstarch step trips up more people than you'd think. Without it, the peach juices never thicken properly, and you end up with a crisp that's more soup than dessert. It's a small measurement, but it's doing a lot of structural work here.

Another one is overcrowding the peaches. If your slices are piled too thick in a small dish, the middle stays underbaked while the edges overcook. Give the fruit room to spread, which is exactly why the shallow baking dish matters so much here.

And don't judge doneness by time alone. Ovens vary, peaches vary in juiciness, and 30 minutes is a guideline, not a guarantee. Watch for visible bubbling at the edges and a deep golden top. Those two signs matter more than the timer.

### **Ingredient Swaps Worth Knowing**

Peaches out of season, or just not looking great at the store? Frozen peaches work fine here. Thaw them first and drain off the excess liquid, or your filling will turn watery instead of thick. Nectarines are also a solid stand in if you want a slightly firmer bite, and they skip the peeling step entirely since the skin is thinner.

No rolled oats on hand? Quick oats will technically work, but the texture softens more in the oven and you lose some of that chew. If you're out of light brown sugar, dark brown sugar works too. It'll push the flavor slightly toward molasses, which honestly isn't a bad thing in a peach crisp recipe like this one.

Some people ask about gluten free flour. A 1:1 gluten free baking blend does fine in the topping, though the crumb texture ends up a touch more delicate. Worth trying if that's a need in your kitchen, just don't expect an identical bite.

### **Storing and Reheating Leftovers**

Peach crisp rarely lasts long in our house, but if you do have leftovers, cover the dish and store it in the fridge for up to four days. The topping softens a bit over time as it absorbs moisture from the fruit, which is normal and still tastes good.

To bring back some of that crunch, reheat individual portions in the oven or toaster oven at 350°F for about ten minutes rather than the microwave. Microwaving works in a pinch, but it steams the topping instead of crisping it back up.

You can also freeze baked, cooled crisp for up to three months. Wrap it tightly, thaw it in the fridge overnight, then reheat in the oven until warmed through and crisp again on top.

### **Conclusion**

This peach crisp recipe isn't complicated, and it doesn't need to be. Ripe peaches, a simple filling, and a topping that actually gets crispy because the pan and the butter temperature are doing their job. That's the whole formula.

If you make it once with the shallow dish, you'll probably never go back to the deep 8x8 again. The crunch to fruit ratio is just better, and honestly, that's the whole reason this recipe exists.
