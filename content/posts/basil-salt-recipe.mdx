There's a small window every year when your basil plant is at its peak and the first frost is closing in. Once that frost hits, the leaves turn dark and mushy overnight, and all that basil you were growing all summer is gone.

A basil salt recipe is one of the easiest ways to catch that basil before it's too late. It takes two ingredients, a food processor, and about half an hour in the oven. What you end up with is a jar of seasoning that carries the smell and flavor of summer basil through the entire winter.

### **Why Make Basil Salt**

Fresh basil doesn't keep. It wilts in the fridge within days and loses most of its flavor if you try to freeze the leaves whole. Drying it on its own also strips a lot of that bright, sharp smell that makes basil worth growing in the first place.

Salt changes that. Blending the leaves with coarse salt and slow-drying the mixture locks in more of the basil's oils than air-drying alone. What you're left with isn't just dried basil with salt mixed in. It's a seasoning where the two have merged into something you can sprinkle on almost anything.

The classic way to use it is embarrassingly simple. Slice a tomato, sprinkle on some basil salt and a little black pepper, and you've got one of the better snacks you'll eat all week. It also works on eggs, roasted vegetables, popcorn, or anywhere you'd normally reach for plain salt and want a little more going on.

### **What You'll Need**

This basil salt recipe only calls for two core ingredients. The ratio between them is where the flavor comes from.

* 1 1/2 to 2 cups fresh basil leaves, packed
* 1 cup coarse salt (kosher salt or coarse sea salt both work)

That's genuinely it. No oil, no preservatives, nothing else added.

On ratio: a straight 1:1 mix of basil to salt works fine at first, but the basil flavor tends to fade faster over the following months. Using closer to a 2:1 ratio, more basil relative to salt, holds onto that flavor longer once the jar has been sitting in your cupboard for a while.

#### **Equipment**

* A food processor or high-powered blender
* A baking sheet
* Parchment paper
* An airtight container for storage

If you have a dehydrator with silicone mats, that works as a substitute for the oven step. Either method gets you to the same result.

### **How to Make Basil Salt**

#### **Step 1: Harvest and Wash the Basil**

Pick your basil before the first hard frost hits. Even a light frost can start to damage the leaves, so don't wait for the "perfect" moment if the forecast looks rough.

Strip the leaves off the stems. You only want the leaves here, since the stems are woody and won't break down the same way in the food processor. Give the leaves a good wash to remove any dirt or bugs.

#### **Step 2: Dry the Leaves**

Lay the washed basil out on clean kitchen towels and let it air dry for about an hour. You want the leaves fully dry before they hit the food processor. Wet basil clumps instead of blending evenly with the salt.

#### **Step 3: Pulse the Basil and Salt Together**

Add your basil leaves and coarse salt to the food processor. Pulse for about 30 seconds, then stop and scrape down the sides if needed.

Continue pulsing for another minute or so. You're not making a paste. You're breaking the leaves down into small flecks that are evenly distributed through the salt. It should look loose and slightly damp, not wet.

#### **Step 4: Spread It Out**

Line a baking sheet with parchment paper. Spread the basil salt mixture across it in a single, even layer, making sure to get the mixture into the corners of the pan.

An even layer matters here. Thick clumps take longer to dry and can stay damp in the middle even after the thinner parts are ready.

#### **Step 5: Dry It in the Oven**

Preheat your oven to 225°F. Place the tray in the oven and let the mixture dry for about 30 minutes.

You're not baking this, you're dehydrating it. The goal is to pull all the remaining moisture out of the basil so the finished salt stores well without clumping or growing mold. Keep an eye on it toward the end since ovens vary.

#### **Step 6: Cool, Crumble, and Store**

Let the tray cool completely before touching the basil salt. It has a tendency to clump slightly as it cools, so break it up with your fingers if needed.

Once it's fully cooled and loose, transfer it into an airtight container. A jar that's properly sized for the amount you made works better than one that's too big, since extra air space can lead to moisture building up over time.

### **Tips for the Best Basil Salt Recipe**

A few small details make a real difference here.

Use coarse salt, not fine table salt. Kosher salt and coarse sea salt both hold up better in the food processor and give you a better texture in the final product. Fine salt tends to clump and doesn't distribute as evenly.

Make sure the basil is completely dry before it goes into the food processor. Any leftover water from washing will throw off the drying time in the oven and can lead to a damp, clumpy result.

Don't rush the oven step. Pulling it out too early leaves moisture behind, and that moisture is what eventually causes mold in stored herbs. When in doubt, give it a few extra minutes.

Store your finished basil salt in a cool, dark cupboard rather than somewhere warm or sunny. Heat and light both break down the herb's oils faster, which is exactly what you're trying to avoid.

### **How to Use Basil Salt**

Once you've got a jar made, using it is the easy part.

Sprinkle it over sliced tomatoes with a little black pepper for a snack that barely takes a minute to put together. Use it in place of regular salt on scrambled eggs, roasted potatoes, or grilled vegetables. It also works well stirred into softened butter for a quick herb butter, or mixed into olive oil as a dip for bread.

Because the basil is already blended into the salt, you get flavor and seasoning in one step instead of measuring out fresh herbs every time you cook.

### **How Long Does Basil Salt Last**

Stored in an airtight container in a cool spot, basil salt keeps well for several months, easily carrying you through the winter until fresh basil is back in season. The salt acts as a natural preservative, and because the moisture has been fully removed, there's little risk of spoilage as long as the container stays sealed and dry.

You will notice the basil flavor mellow slightly the longer it sits, which is normal. That's exactly why leaning toward more basil than salt in your ratio pays off over time.

### **Conclusion** 

This basil salt recipe isn't complicated, and that's the whole appeal. Two ingredients, one food processor, and half an hour in the oven turn a basil plant that's about to die in the frost into a seasoning you'll reach for all winter.

If you've got basil in your garden right now, don't let it go to waste. A jar of homemade basil salt costs you almost nothing to make and does more for your cooking than a lot of the bottled seasonings sitting in your pantry.
