If your basil plant is taking over the garden right now, you're not alone. Most people hit peak basil season with more leaves than they know what to do with, and pesto only uses so much of it before you're sick of the stuff.

These four basil recipes go a different direction. No pesto in sight. Just a dressing, a stuffed mushroom appetizer, a copycat marinara sauce, and a dump-and-bake chicken dinner, all built around actually using up a big pile of fresh leaves instead of letting them wilt on the counter.

## **Why These Basil Recipes Work for Summer**

Basil doesn't keep. Once it's picked, you've got days before it turns dark and bitter, not weeks. That's the whole reason to have more than one basil recipe on hand during the months your plant is producing faster than you can eat it.

Each of these uses basil differently. One blends it raw into a dressing. One folds it into a cheese filling. One infuses a whole sprig into a simmering sauce. One gets chopped fresh into a marinade. Between the four of them, you'll use a genuinely large amount of basil, and you won't be eating pesto for the fifth week running.

### **The Basil Recipes**

#### **Basil Vinaigrette**

This is the recipe to reach for if you've got a truly overwhelming amount of basil, because it uses two full cups of tightly packed leaves, roughly four ounces. That's a lot, and it's exactly the point.

Dressings are forgiving. You're already working with vinegar, oil, and other seasonings, so a slightly imprecise measurement of basil isn't going to throw anything off. Taste as you go and adjust from there.

##### **Ingredients**:

* 2 cups tightly packed fresh basil leaves (about 4 ounces)
* 2 tablespoons chopped sweet onion (or 1 shallot)
* 1 clove garlic
* 1/2 to 3/4 teaspoon salt, to taste
* A pinch of crushed red pepper flakes, to taste
* 2 tablespoons red wine vinegar
* 3 to 4 ounces extra virgin olive oil

##### **Instructions**:

Add the basil to a blender or food processor. Add the onion, garlic, salt, and red pepper flakes. Start with less red pepper than you think you want. It's easy to add more, hard to take it back out once it's blended in.

Pour in the red wine vinegar and olive oil. Blend for about a minute, until everything is fully incorporated and no basil leaves are visible. Taste it. This is the step people skip, and it's the one that actually matters. Adjust the salt and heat level before serving.

Pour it over greens for an instant salad, or use it as a marinade for grilled chicken. It keeps in the fridge for about a week, though the color fades a bit after the first few days.

#### **Cheese and Basil Stuffed Mushrooms**

These come together in two short bakes, and the basil gets folded into a cheese filling instead of sitting raw on top, which means the flavor cooks into the mushroom instead of just sitting on it.

Skip the breadcrumbs most stuffed mushroom recipes call for. They're not necessary here, and leaving them out keeps this naturally gluten free without changing the texture in any way that matters.

##### **Ingredients**:

* 8 ounces white mushrooms or baby portobellas
* 1/4 cup mayonnaise
* 1/2 cup shredded Italian cheese blend
* 1/4 cup lightly packed basil leaves, chopped
* A spoonful of grated parmesan
* A few cracks of black pepper

##### **Instructions**:

Wipe the mushrooms clean with a damp paper towel and pop out the stems. Place them on a lightly greased baking sheet, stem-side down, and bake at 375 degrees for 8 to 10 minutes. This step releases some of their liquid before you stuff them, so the caps don't turn watery later.

While the mushrooms bake, combine the mayonnaise, shredded cheese, basil, parmesan, and pepper in a food processor. Blend until the cheese breaks down and the basil is fully chopped through the mixture. You're not going for a smooth puree, just an evenly distributed filling.

Fill each mushroom cap with about a teaspoon of the mixture and return them to the oven for another 10 minutes, until golden brown on top. The parmesan brings enough salt on its own, so there's no need to add more to the filling.

#### **Copycat Marinara Sauce**

This one uses fresh basil instead of dried, which changes the whole character of the sauce compared to something out of a jar. It costs a couple of dollars to make and stretches further than most store-bought marinara, which usually runs eight to ten dollars for a 24-ounce jar.

##### **Ingredients**:

* 1/4 cup olive oil
* 1/2 medium onion, diced
* A few cloves of garlic, minced
* Salt and pepper, to taste
* 1 teaspoon dried oregano
* 1 (28-ounce) can whole peeled plum tomatoes
* 1 large sprig fresh basil, plus more chopped basil to finish

##### **Instructions**:

Heat the olive oil over medium heat, then add the diced onion. Sauté for 8 to 10 minutes, dropping the heat to medium-low once it starts to sizzle. You want the onion soft and translucent, not browned.

Add the garlic and cook for about a minute, just until fragrant. Season with salt and pepper, then stir in the oregano. Add the tomatoes, breaking them up with a spoon or potato masher as you go. You don't need them fully smooth at this stage.

Bring the sauce up to a bubble, then drop it to a simmer with the lid slightly ajar so steam can escape. Add the whole basil sprig now. It'll infuse the sauce as it cooks and will likely darken, which is fine since you're pulling it out later. Let it simmer for 45 minutes to an hour, stirring occasionally and mashing the tomatoes down further as it thickens.

Fish out the basil sprig before serving. If you want a smoother sauce, use an immersion blender to pulse it a few times, just enough to break down the remaining chunks without turning it into a puree. Taste and adjust the salt one more time before serving over gluten-free pasta, on pizza, or as a dipping sauce.

#### **Dump and Bake Basil Chicken**

This is the easiest recipe of the four. Mix a marinade, pour it over chicken breasts, and bake. There's genuinely not much room to get it wrong.

##### **Ingredients**:

* 1 to 2 pounds chicken breast
* Salt and pepper
* 1/2 cup chicken broth (or white wine)
* 1/4 cup olive oil
* 1/4 cup Dijon mustard
* 1 clove garlic (or 1 teaspoon garlic powder)
* 1 teaspoon Italian seasoning
* 1/4 cup fresh basil, chopped

##### **Instructions**:

Season the chicken breasts with salt and pepper on both sides, then arrange them in a greased baking dish large enough to hold a bit of liquid.

Whisk together the chicken broth, olive oil, Dijon mustard, garlic, Italian seasoning, and chopped basil. Pour the mixture over the chicken. If you want to make this ahead of time, cover the dish and refrigerate for six to eight hours before baking, which deepens the flavor without any extra work on your part.

Bake at 375 degrees for about 30 minutes, until the internal temperature hits 165 degrees. There's no need to baste it partway through. The marinade does its job on its own. The mustard gives the finished chicken a sharp little pop that plays well against the basil underneath it.

## **Conclusion**

Four basil recipes, zero pesto, and a real use for a plant that's producing faster than you can keep up with. The vinaigrette handles the biggest volume of leaves if that's your main problem. The stuffed mushrooms and marinara both fold basil into something you'd make anyway. The chicken wraps up dinner with almost no active effort.

Pick whichever fits what's in your kitchen tonight. None of them require special equipment, and all four use ingredients you likely already have on hand or can pick up cheaply. That's the real advantage of basil recipes like these. They don't ask you to build your week around them. They just use what you've already got growing on the windowsill.
